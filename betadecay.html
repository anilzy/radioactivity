Beta Decay (β-decay)

What is Beta Decay?

Beta decay is a type of radioactive decay in which a nucleus transforms a neutron into a proton or a proton into a neutron, emitting a beta particle in the process.

Types of Beta Decay

1. Beta-minus (β⁻) decay:

In β⁻ decay, a neutron is converted into a proton, and an electron (β⁻ particle) and an antineutrino (ν̄ₑ) are emitted.

Equation:

n → p⁺ + e⁻ + ν̄ₑ

Example: ¹⁴C → ¹⁴N + e⁻ + ν̄ₑ

2. Beta-plus (β⁺) decay (positron emission):

In β⁺ decay, a proton is converted into a neutron, and a positron (β⁺ particle) and a neutrino (νₑ) are emitted.

Equation:

p⁺ → n + e⁺ + νₑ

Example: ¹¹C → ¹¹B + e⁺ + νₑ

Illustrations of Beta Decay

Key Points:

  • Beta decay changes the **atomic number** of a nucleus by ±1 but the **mass number remains the same**.
  • Beta-minus emits an electron and antineutrino; beta-plus emits a positron and neutrino.
  • β⁺ decay can only occur in proton-rich nuclei, β⁻ decay in neutron-rich nuclei.
  • Beta particles are more penetrating than alpha particles but less than gamma rays.
